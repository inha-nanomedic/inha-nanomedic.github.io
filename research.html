Research Details

Expanded descriptions of each research program, outlining the scientific rationale, methodology, and clinical significance.

01 — Bio-adhesive Hydrogel for Hemostasis

Uncontrolled hemorrhage remains a leading cause of preventable death in surgical and trauma settings. Our laboratory designs rapidly crosslinking hydrogel formulations that adhere to wet tissue surfaces within seconds, forming a mechanically robust seal capable of withstanding arterial pressure. These bio-glues leverage catechol chemistry and dual-network architectures to achieve simultaneous tissue adhesion and hemostatic action.

Current efforts focus on tuning gelation kinetics for intraoperative use and incorporating antimicrobial agents to prevent post-surgical infection. Biocompatibility and biodegradation studies in small and large animal models guide our path toward clinical translation.

02 — Radioactive Microspheres for Embolization

Transarterial radioembolization (TARE) delivers high-dose internal radiation selectively to hepatic tumors while sparing healthy parenchyma. We fabricate polymeric microspheres loaded with therapeutic radioisotopes such as yttrium-90 and holmium-166, optimizing particle size distribution to match the microvasculature of tumor-feeding arteries and maximize intratumoral retention.

Our research addresses key challenges including radionuclide encapsulation efficiency, microsphere stability under sterilization conditions, and real-time imaging compatibility for treatment verification. Preclinical catheter-based delivery studies confirm selective tumor uptake and dose-dependent regression in hepatocellular carcinoma models.

03 — miRNA Delivery for Cancer Therapy

MicroRNAs serve as master regulators of gene expression, and their dysregulation is a hallmark of cancer progression. Our miRNA delivery platform uses hydrogel carriers engineered for sustained, localized release of tumor-suppressive miRNA mimics directly into the tumor microenvironment. High-throughput screening identifies candidate miRNAs that silence key oncogenic pathways while minimizing off-target effects.

Chemical modifications enhance miRNA stability against nuclease degradation, and endosomal escape motifs promote efficient cytoplasmic release. This Regulomics-driven approach enables combination therapies targeting multiple nodes in oncogenic signaling cascades simultaneously.

04 — Protein/Peptide Oral Delivery

Oral administration of therapeutic proteins and peptides, such as insulin, GLP-1, and octreotide, is severely limited by enzymatic degradation in the gastrointestinal tract and poor permeability across the intestinal epithelium. We develop multifunctional nanoparticle systems incorporating cell-penetrating peptides (CPPs), mucoadhesive polymers, and enteric coatings to protect payloads and enhance transepithelial absorption.

Tight junction modulation strategies reversibly open paracellular pathways, enabling macromolecular transport without compromising mucosal barrier integrity. Pharmacokinetic studies demonstrate oral bioavailability improvements of several fold compared to unformulated peptides, bringing needle-free biologics delivery closer to clinical reality.

05 — Bone Regeneration via Protein Delivery

Critical-size bone defects arising from trauma, tumor resection, or congenital malformation require osteoinductive stimuli beyond the body's innate repair capacity. Our approach encapsulates bone morphogenetic proteins (BMPs) and other osteogenic growth factors within biodegradable PLGA microspheres, enabling controlled local release that sustains therapeutic concentrations at the defect site over several weeks.

Composite scaffolds combining these microspheres with osteoconductive calcium phosphate ceramics provide both the biological signals and structural framework necessary for vascularized bone regeneration. In vivo studies in calvarial and segmental defect models confirm dose-dependent new bone formation with minimal ectopic ossification.

06 — Drug-Eluting Stent Development

In-stent restenosis, caused by neointimal hyperplasia following stent deployment, remains a significant complication in both vascular and biliary interventions. We engineer biodegradable polymer coatings applied to metallic stent platforms that release antiproliferative agents such as paclitaxel and sirolimus in a controlled, zero-order fashion over clinically relevant time frames.

Coating uniformity, drug-polymer compatibility, and degradation-mediated release kinetics are characterized using accelerated in vitro models. Long-term patency studies in porcine coronary and biliary models demonstrate reduced neointimal thickness and improved lumen preservation compared to bare-metal stents, with complete coating resorption eliminating chronic polymer-related inflammation.
